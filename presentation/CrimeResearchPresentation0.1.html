Spatial Distribution of Crime in Chicago

Collaborative Research Project

Created by Fabian Bohnenberger and Sebastian Martinez

Crime in Chicago

Research Aim

Analyze the relationship between the location of reported crimes and the location and demarcation of police power (police stations and districts)

Research question:
Does the physical location of police stations and the organizational demarcation of police districts have a statistically significant effect on the spatial distribution of crime in the city of Chicago?

Data Collection

Data source is the Data Portal of the City of Chicago

  • Crime Data (2012)
  • Polics District and Community Border Data
  • Socioeconomic (Census) Data

Absolute and Relative Distance

Two distances: Crime scene to (1) the calculated district center and (2) the police station

Two ways to express the distance: (1) absolute distance and (2) relative distance, i.e. the distance of the crime to the center/station divided by the max distance (calculated on a district level)

Inferential Statistics

"Close" and "Far" Crimes Dummy

To determine whether the location of the police station has an effect on crime, we create a dummy variable.

We draw a circle around the police station with a radius of 2/3 the maximum distance.

All crimes in the circle are considered "Close", all outside "Far".

Regression: Model 1.1

##VIOLENT dummy
violent_probit1 <- glm(
    far ~ as.integer(hardship_index) + as.factor(violent), 
    family=binomial(link="probit"), data=Crime_Data
    )

## Creating Fitted Values for the Regression 
fitted <- with(
    Crime_Data, 
    data.frame(hardship_index = mean(as.integer(hardship_index)),
    violent = factor(0:1))
    ) 
fitted$predicted <- predict(violent_probit1, newdata = fitted, 
    type = 'response', interval='confidence'
    )
This is repeated for PROPERTY and MONEY dummies in model 1.2 and 1.3.
